MVP concept · women's T-shirts · brand-specific fit advice

Stop guessing your size.

This demo shows how a small, focused MVP can test whether women are willing to enter their body measurements in order to find the right T-shirt size by brand faster. No full-body scan, no complex platform, just one promise: less sizing stress.

1 category Women's T-shirts, because the scope is small enough for an initial test.
5 questions Only the minimum input needed for an initial fit estimate.
30 sec From input to sizing advice, so the experience stays light and useful.

Why this problem really hurts

A size S is not always a size S. Fit depends on the brand, cut, stretch, and body proportions. That makes people guess, and guessing leads to doubt and returns.

What MVP 1 tests

  • Do people want to enter their measurements?
  • Do they feel helped by brand-specific advice?
  • Do they trust a simple fit estimate enough to buy?

The MVP in 4 steps

Keep it deliberately small. You want evidence, not a perfect system.

1
Start with one product Choose women's T-shirts as the first category. It is simple and recognizable for users.
2
Ask only core measurements Height, bust, shoulders, preferred fit, and current size at known brands are enough for a first test.
3
Give brand advice Show a simple recommendation per brand, such as runs small, true to size, or runs large.
4
Measure confidence Ask afterward whether the user would follow the advice. That is your first signal for product-market fit.

Brand data you will eventually need

The real product becomes powerful once you can map relevant garment measurements to body measurements per brand and per item. For the first version, you do not need all of that data yet.

Product size Bust width, shoulder width, length, fit, and stretch of the item.
Body data The measurements the customer enters themselves or later captures automatically.
Matching logic A simple rules engine that translates into advice: small, regular, or roomy.
